FAQ

What is an Air Quality Egg?
An Air Quality Egg (AQE) is a low-cost sensor system which enables every person to get information about air quality in his direct surroundings. The air quality data is measured by different sensors.
How does the data collection use AQEs work?
The AQE is a sensor system composed of two components: outdoor sensors and a base station (the egg itself). Using an RF transmitter, the sensor data is going to be sent wirelessly to the base station, which should be located inside. The measured air quality data is sent to Cosm (open source data platform to store and access data) in real-time.
Which sensors are available for AQEs?
The standard equipment includes an NO2 sensor and a CO Sensor as well as temperature and humidity sensors. Optionally, additional sensors can be purchased at an extra charge, like PM10 or ozone/O3.
To which target can the AQE data be used?
Because the measured concentrations of NO2 and CO are most responsible for the air pollution in urban areas, the collected data can help supporting the improvement of air quality, since areas which are particulary affected by air pollution can be located much easier.
What are the advantages of using AQEs?
With an air quality egg every person has the opportunity to participate in the conversation about air quality and learn about the current as well as long-term air quality in his near surroundings.
What is LANUV?

LANUV is an abbreviation for "Landesamt für Natur, Umwelt und Verbraucherschutz NRW" (The North Rhine-Westphalia State Environment Agency). The LANUV maintains a large number of stations for measuring air quality, which are eqipped with additional sensors for measuring air quality.

For further information please have a look here.

Which values are being measured?

For the air quality egg data following measurements are available:

Temperature, humidity, nitrogen dioxide and carbon monoxide.

Since the eggs could be located inside or outside a building, the measured values could differ considerably, even though there is only a short distance between two neighbouring eggs.

Temperature is measured in degree Celsius (°C) and Humidity is measured in percent (%).

The NO2 and CO concentrations are both measured in parts per billion(ppb).

Nitrogen dioxide (NO2) is produced as an intermediate in the industrial synthesis of nitric acid, millions of tons of which are produced each year. As it is a toxic gas, it is one of the most important air pollutants.
Further information can be found here.

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a chemical compound of carbon and oxygen. In higher concentrations it is toxic to humans and animals. It can for example result from the incomplete combustion of carbonaceous materials.
Further information can be found here.

In addition to this the LANUV data include following measurements:

Nitrogen monoxide, sulfur dioxide, dust(particulates), ozone and wind velocity.

What is meant by validation and how does it work?

In our case validation basically means finding and labeling outliers in the data we get from LANUV and from the AQEs. This means no data will be deleted or changed.

It is also important to note that measurements labeled as outliers are not always errors in the measurements process. They may be valid observations indicating unusual events with unusual values which are labeled by the validation algorithm.

Outlier detection is done using the R statistics software.

It basically works like this:

First of all the running median of a set of non-validated measurements is calculated. The window size depends on the used data (e.g. LANUV or AQE).

For the validation itself we use a leftsided window ranging from xi to xi-window_size. From the values inside this window we compute the interquartile range. Starting from the most recent measurement we check if the measured value is smaller than the running median − 1,5 × interquartile range or larger than the running median + 1,5 × interquartile range. If that is the case the measurement is labeled as an outlier.

The validation of the LANUV data runs daily at 1:42 AM (UTC+2) and the validation of the COSM data daily at 12:22 PM (UTC+2).

How do I view a table of the data?

To view a table of the data, click on one of the corresponding symbols below the map visualizing the different sensors.

In the table view you can choose a measurement period. The table then shows all the values which have been measured in this period of time by the different sensors. You can distinguish validated and non-validated data and easily detect outliers by the background color of the cell.

How do I view a diagram of the data?

To view a diagram of the data, click on one of the corresponding symbols below the map visualizing the different sensors.

In the diagram view you have the possibility to select a measurement period, as well as one, or for comparison purposes two different attributes (e.g. to get information about the relation between temperature and humidity).
You can choose between predefined measurement periods or a custom range.

How do I download the data?

There are two ways to download the data you want.

If you are already experienced in using a Sensor Observation Service you can write own requests to our SOS
http://giv-geosoft2d.uni-muenster.de/istsos/lanuv?service=SOS for LANUV data or
http://giv-geosoft2d.uni-muenster.de/istsos/cosmcosm?service=SOS for COSM data.
You can find further information on how to do this here


The easy but very basic way to download data is clicking the download button and selecting the service (e.g. LANUV or Air Quality Egg) and the measuring station you want. Then you only need to choose the observed property you want and a time frame. After clicking submit your can select the output format you prefer. A getObservation request is automatically generated and you get your data.

Getting started

Chapter 1: The starting screen

At first you see the starting screen, which appears as the standard view after opening the web page.

The screen of the web page is basically divided in three elements.

In the center of the screen you can see a map(1) based on Open Street Map, on which measuring stations are visualized.

In the upper part of the page, a selection menu(2) is available, including the items Download, Getting Started, FAQ and About.

In the lower part you can see a sensor bar(3), which is used for the visualization of currently measured values of the selected station.


In order to download data, click on the menu item "Download" and follow the instructions in the download manager.

If you have questions to some topics of the project or you like to get some background information, click on the menu item "FAQ".
Then a list appears with frequently asked questions concerning this project.

By clicking on each question its answer appears.

Click the exit button "x" to exit the window and you will return to the starting screen.

The menu item "About" gives a short overview of the project and the possibility to contact us. To have a look at it, click on the about button.
A window will appear.

Click the exit button "x" and the window will disappear again.

Chapter 2: Map functions

The map view provides several functions.

In order to zoom into the map, click on the "+" button.

In the same way you can click on the "-" button to zoom out of the map.

Alternatively you can use the scroll wheel of your computer mouse to zoom in and out of the map.

To move the map, you can click the left mouse button and move it in a direction you like to.

As you can see, several different objects are displayed on the map.

The circles containing a number(1), describe a clustering of symbols. A cluster concentrates various symbols, which are in close proximity. The different colour results out of the fact that a clustering of up to 9 symbols is visualized by a green circle, 10 or more clustered symbols by a yellow circle.

As an alternative to a direct zoom into the map, a click on the circle also causes a change in scale.

You can choose two different types of symbols on the map.

The icon in shape of an egg(2) refers to air quality egg data measuring stations, which have been placed there, the icon in shape of a radio tower(3) refers to LANUV stations.

You can click on each of the icons. After doing so, currently measured values of the selected station(2) will be displayed below the map(4).


Chapter 3: The AQE sensor bar

After clicking on a symbol (either air quality eggs or LANUV stations) you notice that in the sensor bar in the lower part of the page some values appeared.
They show the most recent values measured by the selected station.

If you like to view additional information about the selected station (e.g. metadata,tables and diagrams), click on one of the icons in the lower part of the map.
Here you have the choice to select any of the symbols.
Each symbol will open the same window with the same functionalities.

If you click on one of the icons without having selected a measuring station before, an error message will appear, giving you the advice to select a measuring station.

As it is handled differently, the further steps after choosing an air quality egg or LANUV stations will be explained separately.

In the case you first chose an air quality egg and clicked on a symbol in the sensor bar, a window opens, which contains three pages ("Home", "Table" and "Diagram").

As a default, you see a page called "Home", where you can view some metadata about the egg you selected.

There are also links available, leading you to the Cosm home page.
One for the creator (providing you with current data from all the air quality eggs which are available), another for the Cosm feed (providing you with current data from the egg you chose).


In the table section you can see a table of the data, comprising all the sensor data available from the egg.

You can choose a period of time, from which you like to view data by clicking on one of the predefined time periods or enter a custom period.

Each data value you can see for the different sensors is marked whether it is rawdata, an outlier or no outlier.
You can distinguish them by the different colors used for the different categories.


In the diagram view you also can choose a period of time.

Furthermore you can choose the attributes (either one or two different), which should be displayed.
If you select two different attributes, it is easier to understand relations between attribute values.

Chapter 4: The LANUV sensor bar

In case you first chose the a LANUV station, you will notice that additional symbols will be displayed in the sensor bar.
That is because the LANUV stations are equipped with additional sensors for measuring air quality.

Here, in the same way as described before, you can click on any symbol in the sensor bar and again a window opens.

The window for the LANUV stations is structured in the same way as it has been for the air quality eggs ("Home", "Table" and "Diagram").

You may notice that the metadata will differ and that there is a picture added of each station.

There are also links, which will lead you to the LANUV homepage, providing you with further information about the station and the measurements.

The table- and the diagram view can be used in the same way, except that there are additional values, which can be visualized.

About

Description

qualitySCHU is a project developed by students at ifgi, Münster, which allows everyone to retrieve and visualize air quality data captured by so called air quality eggs, as well as data provided by a german state agency called LANUV. The project name qualitySCHU refers to the egyptian deity called Schu. In the Egyptian mythology Schu was considered as a personification of air.
