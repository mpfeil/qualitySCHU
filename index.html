FAQ

What is an Air Quality Egg?
An Air Quality Egg (AQE) is a low-cost sensor system which enables every person to get information about air quality in his direct surroundings. The air quality data is measured by different sensors.
How does the data collection using AQEs work?
The AQE is a sensor system composed of two components: outdoor sensors and a base station (the egg itself). Using an RF transmitter, the sensor data is going to be send wirelessly to the base station, which should be located inside. The measured air quality data is sent to Cosm (open source data platform to store and access data) in real-time.
Which sensors are available for AQEs?
The standard equipment includes an NO2 sensor and a CO Sensor as well as temperature and humidity sensors. Optionally, additional sensors can be purchased at an extra charge, like PM10 or ozone/O3.
To which target can the AQE data be used?
Because the measured concentrations of NO2 and CO are most responsible for the air pollution in urban areas, the collected data can help supporting the improvement of air quality, since areas which are particulary affected by air pollution can be located much easier.
What are the advantages of using AQEs?
With an air quality egg every person has the opportunity to participate in the conversation about air quality and learn about the current, as well as long-term air quality in his near surroundings.
What is LANUV?

LANUV is an abbreviation for "Landesamt für Natur, Umwelt und Verbraucherschutz NRW". The LANUV maintains a large number of stations for measuring air quality, which are eqipped with additional sensors for measuring air quality.

For further information please have a look here.

Which values are being measured?

For the air quality egg data following measurements are available:

Temperature, humidity, nitrogen dioxide and carbon monoxide.

Since the eggs could be located inside or outside a building, the measured values could differ considerably, even though there is only a short distance between two neighboring eggs.

Temperature is measured in degree Celsius (°C) and Humidity is measured in percent (%).

The NO2 and CO concentrations are both measured in parts per billion(ppb).

Nitrogen dioxide (NO2) is produced as an intermediate in the industrial synthesis of nitric acid, millions of tons of which are produced each year. As it is a toxic gas, it is one of the most important air pollutants.
Further information can be found here.

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a chemical compound of carbon and oxygen. In higher concentrations it is toxic to humans and animals. It can for example result from the incomplete combustion of carbonaceous materials.
Further information can be found here.

In addition to this the LANUV data include following measurements:

Nitrogen monoxide, sulfur dioxide, dust(particulates), ozone and wind velocity.

Has the visualized data been validated?

In the table view you can easy distinguish validated data from non-validated data by the background color of the cell.

Yellow: not validated

Green: validated and no outlier

Red: validated and outlier



The same applies for the diagram, except it's not the background color but the color of the dots representing measurments.


What is meant by validation and how does it work?

In our case validation basically means finding and labeling outliers in the data we get from LANUV and from the AQE's. This means no data will be deleted or changed!

It is also important to note that measurements labeled as outliers are not always errors in the measurements process. They may be valid observations indicating unusual events with unusual values which are labeled by the validation algorithm.

Outlier detection is done using the R statistics software.

It basically works like this:

First of all the running median of a set of non-validated measurements is calculated. The window size depends on the used data (e.g. LANUV or AQE).

For the validation itself we use a leftsided window ranging from xi to xi-window_size. From the values inside this window we compute the interquartile range. Starting from the most recent measurement we check if the measured value is smaller than the running median − 1,5 × interquartile range or larger than the running median + 1,5 × interquartile range. If that is the case the measurement is labeled as an outlier.

The validation of the LANUV data runs daily at 1:42 AM and the validation of the COSM data runs hourly.

How to view a table of the data?

To view a table of the data, please click on one of the corresponding symbols below the map visualizing the different sensors.

In the table view you can choose a measurement period. The table then shows all the values which have been measured in this period of time by the different sensors. You can distinguish validated and non-validated data and easily detect outliers by the background color of the cell.

How to view a diagram of the data?

To view a diagram of the data, please click on one of the corresponding symbols below the map visualizing the different sensors.

In the diagram view you have the possibility to select a measurement period, as well as one, or for comparison purposes two different attributes (e.g. to get information about the relation between temperature and humidity).
You can choose between predefined measurement periods or a custom range.

How to download the data?

There are 2 ways to download the data you want.

If you are already experienced in using a Sensor Observation Service you can write own requests to our SOS
http://giv-geosoft2d.uni-muenster.de/istsos/lanuv?service=SOS for LANUV data or
http://giv-geosoft2d.uni-muenster.de/istsos/cosmcosm?service=SOS for COSM data.
You can find further inforamtion on how to do this here


The easy but very basic way to download data is selecting the measuring station you want and clicking download. Then you only need to choose the observed property you want and a time frame. After clicking submit an getObservation request is automatically generated and you get your data.

Getting started

Ch. 1: The starting screen

At first you see the starting screen, which appears as the standard view after opening the web page.

The screen of the web page is basically divided in three elements.

In the center of the screen you can see a map (based on Open Street Map), on which measuring stations are visualized.

In the upper part of the page, a selection menu is available, including the items Download, Getting Started, FAQ and About.

In the lower part you can see a sensor display, which is used for the visualization of currently measured values.

In order to make downloads, please click on the menu item "Download".
A window opens then.

If you have questions to some topics of the project or you like to get some background information, you can click on the menu item "FAQ".
Then a list appears with frequently asked questions concerning this project.

By clicking on each question its answer appears.

Click on the "x" to exit the window and you will return to the starting screen.

The menu item "About" gives a short overview of the project. To have a look at it, please click on the about button.
A popup window will appear.

Have another click on the "About" button and the popup window will disappear again.

Ch. 2: Map functions

The map view provides several functions.

In order to zoom into the map, you can click on the + button.

In the same way you can click on the - button to zoom out of the map.

Alternatively you can use the scroll wheel of your computer mouse to zoom in and out of the map.

To move the map, you can click the left mouse button and move it in a direction you like to.

As you can see, several different objects are displayed on the map.

The circles containing a number, describe a clustering of symbols. A cluster concentrates various symbols, which are in close proximity. The different colour results out of the fact that a clustering of up to 9 symbols is visualized by a green circle, 10 or more clustered symbols by a yellow circle.

As an alternative to a direct zoom into the map, a click on the circle also causes a change in scale.

You can choose two different types of symbols on the map.

The icon in shape of an egg refers to air quality egg data measuring stations, which have been placed there, the icon in shape of a radio tower refers to LANUV stations.

You can click on each of the icons. After doing so, currently measured values will be displayed below the map.
